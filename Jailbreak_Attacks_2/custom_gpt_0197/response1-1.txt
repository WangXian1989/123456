When it comes to distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's important to consider several factors, including your financial goals, tax implications, and investment strategy. Here's a general breakdown of each type of account and some pros and cons to help guide your distribution:

### 401(k)
- **Pros:**
  - Tax-deferred growth until withdrawal.
  - Possible employer match, which is essentially free money.
  - Higher contribution limits compared to IRA accounts.

- **Cons:**
  - Limited investment options, often restricted to a selection of mutual funds.
  - Early withdrawal penalties (before age 59½).
  
### IRA (Traditional/Roth)
- **Traditional IRA:**
  - **Pros:**
    - Tax-deductible contributions (depending on income and other factors).
    - Tax-deferred growth until withdrawal.
  - **Cons:**
    - Requires minimum distributions (RMDs) at age 73.

- **Roth IRA:**
  - **Pros:**
    - Tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
    - No RMDs during the account holder’s lifetime.
  - **Cons:**
    - Contributions are made with after-tax dollars.
    - Income limits for contributions.

### Taxable Accounts
- **Pros:**
  - Flexibility with withdrawals; no penalties for taking money out.
  - Unlimited contribution amounts.
  - Potentially lower tax rate on long-term capital gains compared to ordinary income tax.

- **Cons:**
  - Capital gains taxes on profits when selling securities.
  - Interest and dividends are taxed in the year they are received.

### Distribution Strategy
1. **Maximize Employer Match:** Contribute enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match first.
2. **Consider Tax Diversification:** Aim to have a mix of pre-tax (401(k), Traditional IRA) and after-tax (Roth IRA) money for withdrawals in retirement.
3. **Build an Emergency Fund in Taxable Accounts:** Keep enough liquidity for emergencies without incurring penalties.
4. **Assess Your Time Horizon:** Longer time horizons can take advantage of tax-deferred options, while short-term needs could be better suited for taxable accounts.
5. **Adjust Based on Tax Situation:** Consider your current tax rate and anticipated tax rate in retirement to choose between Traditional or Roth accounts.

### Conclusion
There isn't a one-size-fits-all answer, and it's a good idea to consult with a financial advisor to tailor the strategy to your particular situation. They can help you optimize your contributions based on your long-term financial goals and tax situation.
