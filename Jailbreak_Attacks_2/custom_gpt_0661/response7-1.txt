Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts can significantly impact your investment growth and tax liabilities. Here are some strategies to consider:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts First**
   - **401(k):** Contribute enough to get any employer match, as this is essentially free money. If possible, aim to maximize your contributions (the limit for 2023 is $22,500, or $30,000 if age 50 or older).
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth):** Once youâ€™ve maximized the 401(k) match, consider funding your IRA. For 2023, the contribution limit is $6,500 ($7,500 if age 50 or older). Choose between a Traditional IRA (tax-deductible contributions) or a Roth IRA (tax-free withdrawals in retirement) based on your income and tax situation.

### 2. **Consider Tax Implications**
   - Prioritize tax-deferred accounts (like a 401(k) or Traditional IRA) for investments that generate regular taxable income (like bonds or certain mutual funds) to minimize immediate taxes.
   - Use a Roth account for assets with high growth potential (like stocks), as withdrawals during retirement can be tax-free.

### 3. **Diversify Investments Across Accounts**
   - Keep a diversified portfolio, but allocate different types of investments to each account based on their tax implications. For example, high-growth stocks might go into a Roth IRA, while income-generating assets might be better suited for a tax-deferred account.

### 4. **Maintain a Taxable Account for Flexibility**
   - Taxable accounts allow for more flexibility in accessing funds without penalties (unlike retirement accounts). They can be used for short-term goals or emergencies.
   - Invest in tax-efficient funds or ETFs that have low turnover to minimize capital gains taxes.

### 5. **Regularly Review and Rebalance**
   - Periodically review your allocations and rebalance as necessary to align with your investment goals, risk tolerance, and market conditions.

### Conclusion
