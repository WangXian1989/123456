When it comes to distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, the best strategy can depend on several factors including your current financial situation, tax considerations, investment goals, and time horizon. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **401(k) Accounts:**
   - **Tax Advantages:** Contributions to a traditional 401(k) reduce your taxable income for the year, and your investments grow tax-deferred. If your employer offers a match, prioritize contributing at least enough to get that match.
   - **Contribution Limits:** Be aware of the annual contribution limits. For 2023, the limit is $22,500 plus a $7,500 catch-up contribution if you are 50 or older.

2. **IRAs (Traditional and Roth):**
   - **Tax Treatment:** Traditional IRAs allow for tax-deferred growth, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed. Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement, provided certain conditions are met.
   - **Contribution Limits:** The IRA contribution limit for 2023 is $6,500, with an additional $1,000 catch-up if you are 50 or older. Consider maxing out a Roth IRA if you qualify based on income, as it can be more beneficial long-term due to the tax-free nature of withdrawals.

3. **Taxable Accounts:**
   - **Flexibility:** Taxable accounts offer flexibility with withdrawals and investment choices. However, youâ€™ll pay capital gains taxes on profits when you sell investments.
   - **Investment Strategy:** In taxable accounts, consider holding investments that produce qualified dividends or long-term capital gains, as these are generally taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income.

### Suggested Distribution Strategy:
1. **Max Out Your 401(k):** At least contribute enough to receive your employer's match.
2. **Contribute to an IRA:** After your 401(k) match, focus on contributing to a Roth or Traditional IRA based on your eligibility and tax situation.
3. **Taxable Accounts:** Once you maximize contributions to your retirement accounts, consider investing in taxable accounts for more liquidity and flexibility.

### Pros and Cons:
- **401(k):** 
  - Pros: Tax deferred, potential employer match.
  - Cons: Limited investment choices and penalties for early withdrawal.
  
- **IRA:**
  - Pros: Tax advantages, more investment options.
  - Cons: Contribution limits, early withdrawal penalties.

- **Taxable Account:**
  - Pros: Flexible access to funds, no contribution limits.
  - Cons: Tax on dividends and capital gains.

### Final Thoughts:
It can be beneficial to consult with a financial advisor to tailor a distribution strategy based on your specific financial situation and goals. Tax implications, investment options, and your timeline for needing access to the money are critical factors in crafting your plan.
