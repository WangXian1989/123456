Distributing your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts involves considering several factors, including your current tax situation, retirement goals, investment options, and risk tolerance. Here are some strategic steps to help you decide:

### 1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
   - **401(k):** 
     - Contribute enough to get any employer match, as this is essentially free money.
     - Consider contributing to a traditional 401(k) for immediate tax benefits or a Roth 401(k) if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **IRA:**
     - If eligible, contribute the maximum to a traditional or Roth IRA, depending on your income level and retirement objectives.

### 2. **Assess Your Tax Situation**
   - **Traditional Accounts (401(k) and Traditional IRA):**
     - Contributions are tax-deductible, offering immediate tax relief. However, withdrawals during retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
   - **Roth Accounts:**
     - While contributions are made with after-tax dollars, qualified withdrawals are tax-free, which can be beneficial if you anticipate a higher tax rate in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts:**
     - Investments held here may be subject to capital gains tax and income tax on dividends, so consider using these accounts for investments that generate less taxable income.

   - *Time Horizon:* Longer timeframes may allow for more aggressive investments, particularly in tax-advantaged accounts.
   - *Diversification:* Ensure a mix of asset classes across all accounts to manage risk effectively.

### 4. **Monitor and Adjust**
   - Regularly review your asset allocation and rebalance as necessary to stay aligned with your investment strategy and goals.

### Example Allocation Strategy:
- **401(k):** Max out employer contribution, focus on growth-oriented investments (e.g., stocks).
- **IRA:** If you qualify, invest in a mix of index funds and bonds, considering a Roth for tax-free growth.
- **Taxable Account:** Use for short-term savings or investments that generate lower taxable income (like municipal bonds).

This structured approach helps you leverage the benefits of each account type while aligning your investments with your financial goals.
