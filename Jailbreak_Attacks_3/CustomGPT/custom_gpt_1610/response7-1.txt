### 1. Understand Each Account Type

- **401(k)**: This employer-sponsored retirement plan offers tax-deferred growth. Contributions are made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income. However, withdrawals during retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
- **IRA (Individual Retirement Account)**: This can be a traditional IRA (tax-deferred) or a Roth IRA (tax-free growth). In a traditional IRA, contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals in retirement will be taxed. Roth contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but withdrawals are generally tax-free in retirement if certain conditions are met.
- **Taxable Accounts**: These accounts do not have tax advantages but offer flexibility to withdraw money at any time without penalties. Capital gains and dividends are taxed in the year they are realized.

### 2. Consider Your Financial Goals

- **Short-Term Needs**: If you anticipate needing access to your funds in the next few years, allocate more to your taxable accounts for liquidity.
- **Retirement Savings**: Prioritize tax-advantaged accounts (401(k) and IRA) for long-term growth and tax benefits. Maximize employer matches in your 401(k) first, then consider contributing to an IRA.

### 3. Factor in Taxes

- Tax implications are crucial when deciding your distributions. For example, if you anticipate your income will be higher in retirement, it may make sense to focus contributions on a Roth IRA now for tax-free withdrawals later.

### 4. Diversify Investments

Regardless of account type, ensure your investment strategy aligns with your risk tolerance and time horizon. Diversifying across asset classes can help balance risk and returns.

### 5. Regularly Reassess and Adjust

Your financial situation, market conditions, and tax laws can change. Review your allocations periodically and adjust them based on performance and any changes in your goals or circumstances.

### Sample Distribution Strategy:

1. **401(k)**: Contribute enough to get any employer match, then consider contributing enough to max your annual limit.
2. **IRA**: Max out a traditional or Roth IRA, depending on your tax situation.
3. **Taxable Accounts**: If you have additional funds after maxing out tax-advantaged accounts, use taxable accounts for flexible investing.

### Conclusion

A well-thought-out distribution strategy among these accounts can optimize tax implications and enhance your long-term financial health. Consider consulting with a financial planner to tailor the strategy to your specific situation.
