1. **Maximize Employer Contributions**: If your 401(k) offers matching contributions from your employer, aim to contribute enough to get the full match. This is essentially free money.

2. **Consider Tax Treatment**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are often pre-tax, which can lower your taxable income. However, withdrawals during retirement will be taxed as ordinary income.
   - **IRA (Traditional or Roth)**: Traditional IRAs allow for pre-tax contributions (also taxed upon withdrawal), while Roth IRAs use after-tax contributions (but allow tax-free withdrawals in retirement). Choose based on your current versus expected future tax rate.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These are subject to capital gains tax upon selling investments. They offer more flexibility but can incur taxes on dividends and interest.

3. **Emergency Fund and Short-Term Needs**: Ensure you have liquid savings for emergencies outside of these accounts. Taxable accounts are more accessible if you need funds sooner.

4. **Long-Term Strategy**:
   - If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute enough to maximize that first.
   - Next, consider contributing to an IRA for the tax advantages.
   - After reaching the limit for retirement accounts, consider investing in a taxable account to benefit from broader investment options and liquidity.

5. **Consider Diversification**: Across all accounts, diversify your investments in terms of asset classes (stocks, bonds, etc.) and sectors to mitigate risk.

6. **Reevaluate Regularly**: Your financial situation and goals may change, so periodically review and adjust your asset allocation and contributions.

7. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: It's often beneficial to speak with a financial planner who can provide personalized advice based on your circumstances.

Balancing these accounts effectively can help maximize growth while managing tax implications, but tailoring your strategy to your specific situation is key.
