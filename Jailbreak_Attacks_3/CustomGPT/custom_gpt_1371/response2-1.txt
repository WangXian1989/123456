When considering how to distribute your money among a 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, here are some strategies to optimize your investments based on various factors including your financial goals, tax situation, and investment horizon:

1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k)**: If your employer offers a matching contribution to your 401(k), prioritize contributing enough to receive the full match. This is essentially free money and can significantly boost your retirement savings.

2. **Consider Tax Advantages**:
   - **401(k)**: Contributions are typically made pre-tax, reducing your taxable income for the year. However, you'll pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Similar to a 401(k), contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals during retirement will be taxed as ordinary income.
   - **Roth IRA**: Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free. This can be beneficial if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
   - **Taxable Accounts**: These do not have tax advantages, but you can withdraw funds at any time without penalty. Consider this for short-term needs or flexibility.

3. **Liquidity Needs**: If you foresee needing access to your money in the short term, prioritize your taxable account. For longer-term goals, tax-advantaged accounts (like 401(k) and IRAs) are generally more beneficial for growth due to tax deferral.

4. **Investment Diversification**: Ensure that your investments across these accounts are diversified. You can hold different assets in each account to optimize tax efficiency while achieving your overall asset allocation.

5. **Tax Implications on Withdrawals**: Be mindful of how withdrawals from each account will be taxed in retirement. Strategically plan your withdrawals to manage your tax bracket effectively.

6. **Consider Contribution Limits**: Be aware of the contribution limits for each account:
   - 401(k): For 2023, the limit is $22,500 (or $30,000 if over age 50).
   - IRA: The limit is $6,500 (or $7,500 if over age 50).

7. **Rebalancing**: Regularly review and rebalance your portfolio as your financial situation, goals, and market conditions change.

8. **Consult a Financial Advisor**: If you're uncertain or have a complex financial situation, it may be worthwhile to consult a financial advisor who can provide personalized advice.
