Generally, people consider factors such as:

1. **Tax Benefits**: Traditional 401(k)s and IRAs offer tax-deferred growth, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Taxable accounts provide more flexibility but may incur capital gains taxes.

2. **Investment Goals**: Clearly define your short-term vs. long-term goals. You might place long-term growth investments in tax-advantaged accounts and keep more liquid assets or conservative investments in taxable accounts.

3. **Risk Tolerance**: Your comfort level with risk can influence where you invest your money. Higher-risk investments might be more suitable for tax-deferred accounts, which can grow without immediate tax implications.

4. **Contribution Limits and Matching**: Maximize any employer match in a 401(k) before contributing to other accounts. 

5. **Withdrawal Flexibility**: Think about how and when you may want to access the funds in the future.
