Distributing your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts is an important financial strategy that can have significant implications for your retirement savings and taxes. Here are some general guidelines to consider:

1. **Maximize Employer Match in 401(k):** If your employer offers a match on your 401(k) contributions, try to contribute at least enough to get the full match, as this is essentially free money.

2. **Consider Contribution Limits:** Be aware of the annual contribution limits for each account:
   - 401(k): In 2023, it's $22,500 (or $30,000 if you're age 50 or older).
   - IRA: In 2023, it's $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're age 50 or older).
   
3. **Tax Advantages:**
   - **401(k):** Contributions are made pre-tax, which lowers your taxable income for the year. However, withdrawals in retirement will be taxed as ordinary income.
   - **IRA:** Traditional IRAs can also provide tax-deductible contributions, while Roth IRAs allow for tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals in retirement (provided certain conditions are met).
   - **Taxable Accounts:** Investments in taxable accounts do not offer tax deferral or deduction benefits, but you have more flexibility in terms of withdrawals.

4. **Investment Strategy:** Depending on your risk tolerance and investment goals, you may choose to invest differently in each account. For instance:
   - Place higher-growth investments (like stocks) in accounts where they can grow tax-deferred (401(k) and IRA).
   - Use taxable accounts for investments that produce income (like bonds) to manage tax implications better.

5. **Withdrawal Strategy:** Consider your future withdrawal strategy. Tax-deferred accounts (401(k) and Traditional IRA) will be taxed upon withdrawal, while Roth accounts allow for tax-free withdrawals. It may benefit you to maintain a mix to manage your tax bracket effectively in retirement.

6. **Emergency Fund:** Ensure that you maintain a liquid emergency fund outside of these accounts to cover unexpected expenses without needing to withdraw from your retirement savings.
