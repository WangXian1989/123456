When determining how to distribute your money among your 401(k), IRA, and taxable accounts, it's essential to consider several factors, including your financial goals, risk tolerance, tax situation, and investment time horizon. Here are some general strategies for effective distribution:

1. **Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: Prioritize contributing to tax-advantaged accounts like your 401(k) and IRA. These accounts offer tax benefits that can help your investments grow more efficiently over time. For example, if your employer offers a match on your 401(k), aim to contribute at least enough to get the full match, as it's essentially free money.

2. **Understand Contribution Limits**: Each account type has specific contribution limits. Ensure you are aware of these limits and plan your contributions accordingly. For 2023, for instance, the maximum contribution limit for a 401(k) is $22,500 (or $30,000 if you're 50 or older), while IRA contributions are limited to $6,500 (or $7,500 if you're 50 or older).

3. **Asset Allocation**: Consider how your investments are allocated across these accounts. For tax-advantaged accounts, you may want to hold more growth-oriented investments (like stocks) that benefit from tax-deferred growth or tax-free growth (in the case of Roth IRAs). In taxable accounts, consider holding more tax-efficient investments (like index funds or ETFs) to minimize capital gains taxes.

4. **Withdrawal Strategy**: Have a plan for how you will withdraw funds in retirement. Generally, it may be advantageous to withdraw from taxable accounts first, followed by tax-deferred accounts (like 401(k)s or traditional IRAs), and finally tax-free accounts (like Roth IRAs).

5. **Periodic Review**: Regularly review and adjust your asset allocation and contributions as needed. Changes in your financial situation, goals, or market conditions may warrant adjustments to your distribution strategy.
